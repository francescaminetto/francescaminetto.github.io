About

I am a political scientist working at the intersection of international institutions, global governance, and European politics. My research examines how international institutions can shape EU policymaking from the outside in, challenging the image of the EU as a unilateral norm exporter and arguing that its deep embeddedness in global governance means it can absorb as much as it exports.

I am currently a Max Weber Postdoctoral Fellow at the Robert Schuman Centre for Advanced Studies at the European University Institute. Before that, I completed my doctoral work at the Hertie School in Berlin, where I was associated with the Jacques Delors Centre.

My research is methodologically distinctive in combining computational and statistical analyses with in-depth qualitative work. My work has appeared in the Journal of European Public Policyand in edited volumes including Policy-Making in the European Union (Oxford University Press). I am currently working on a new edition of The Council of the European Union (Bloomsbury, forthcoming 2027).

Outside research, I contribute to policy debates through policy writing and teaching on EU external action and European governance. I am also one of the founders of the FutureEU Initiative, a student-led platform that seeks to bridge academic knowledge and public debate on the future of the EU. My fieldwork occasionally extends beyond Brussels — when away from the desk, I enjoy foraging for mushrooms and finding four-leaf clovers.

Journal Articles

Does the EU ever import its policies? The overlooked role of international institutions

Minetto, Francesca.

Journal of European Public Policy, 1-35. 2025.

PDF Replication

Prevailing narratives commonly depict the EU as a key actor shaping international norms and a net exporter of policies. However, we know little about how the involvement of the EU in the international arena affects its own policy development. Does the EU ever import policies from international institutions? This study examines the prevalence of policy import within the EU's legal framework, scrutinising the conditions and mechanisms that can facilitate such imports. I analyse the contributory role of compliance with international norms, the existence of successful models, the expectation of competence expansion, and crises in shaping the likelihood of policy import. I test this on a novel dataset comprising 1,194 legislative files introduced post-Lisbon in the European Union between 2008 and 2022. The key descriptive finding is that more than 20 per cent of the legislative files in this period incorporate models and policies from international institutions. This paper systematically analyses the relevance of international commitments across diverse policy areas in EU policy-making, offering valuable insights into this under-explored dimension. It illustrates that international models and commitments have tangible ramifications on EU legislative decision-making, leading to policy change and innovation.

Book Chapters

An Institutional Anatomy and Five Policy Modes

Wallace, Helen, Christine Reh, and Francesca Minetto.

In Policy-Making in the European Union, 9th ed., edited by Helen Wallace, Mark A. Pollack, and Alasdair R. Young. Oxford University Press.

PDF

This chapter examines the European Union's institutional design and how its institutions interact with national institutions in five different policy modes. The chapter first considers the evolving role and internal functioning of the European Commission, Council of the EU, European Council, European Parliament, and Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU). It also discusses quasi-autonomous agencies, in particular the European Central Bank (ECB), institutionalized control and scrutiny, and non-state actors. The chapter then offers an analysis of five policy modes that capture the different patterns of interaction between the EU and its member states: the classical Community method, the regulatory mode, the distributional mode, policy coordination, and intensive transgovernmentalism.

Working Papers

Introducing the EUxII Database: Mapping the Relevance of International Institutions in EU Policymaking

Minetto, Francesca.

Under review

This article presents a comprehensive database designed to address the scholarly gap in understanding policy diffusion and interactions between the European Union and international institutions. The need for more accessible data has hindered systematic analysis of how international institutions feature in EU’s legislative decision-making. This paper introduces the EUxII Database, a novel, hand-curated resource that maps the relevance of over 120 IIs across 1,480 EU legislative files introduced between 2008 and 2022. The database systematically codes when IIs are mentioned, whether these references indicate policy import, and how they intersect with key procedural stages. It also captures contextual factors such as major crises and domestic politicization, and provides verbatim excerpts of II-related references and import. In addition to legislative file-level data, EUxII includes detailed metadata on international institutions themselves—like institutional strength, EU membership overlap, and formal partnerships. To demonstrate its analytical potential, I present an application tracing the temporal dynamics of II mentions and policy import in the post-Lisbon period, alongside an overview of institutional prominence in EU lawmaking. The database is fully linkable to other sources of EU legislative data through COD and CELEX identifiers. By opening up the black box of how global institutions interact with EU policymaking, EUxII provides a new foundation for studying inter-institutional influence, policy diffusion, and the evolving international embeddedness of the EU.

International Institutions as Sources of Policy-Relevant Information for the European Union

Minetto, Francesca, Sebastian Ramirez-Ruiz and Roman Senninger.

In preparation

The European Union is often portrayed as a major exporter of policy ideas and norms, yet the extent to which it relies on information and ideas from external actors remains a less explored aspect of European governance. This paper examines the role of international institutions (IIs) as external information providers in EU policymaking. Drawing on a large-scale citation analysis of over 115,000 EU policy documents published between 2000 and 2024, we analyse how often and which international institutions are referenced across EU policy texts. We find that while EU institutions predominantly cite their own documents, international institutions are the most frequently referenced category of external actors, cited more often than think tanks, academic institutions, or national governments. This pattern of II citation has remained remarkably stable over the past two and a half decades and spans a wide array of policy domains. However, IIs with strong economic regulatory mandates—particularly the OECD, IMF, and World Bank—dominate references, across policy areas and institutions. These findings provide new empirical insight into the external knowledge landscape of EU policy documentation. By mapping citation patterns at scale, the study highlights which international organisations function as prominent reference points in EU policy texts and contributes to broader debates on evidence use, expert authority, and inter-organisational knowledge circulation in global governance. It invites a reassessment of the EU's knowledge politics and challenges the notion of the EU as merely an exporter of policy ideas.

Policy Writing

The European Council: truly the law-maker-in-chief?

Bressanelli, Edoardo, Christel Koop, Francesca Minetto, and Christine Reh.

Centre for European Policy Studies (CEPS)

PDF

We know that the European Council is a powerful and visible strategic agenda-setter, crisis manager, and impasse-breaker. Yet we are less familiar with the role of national heads of state or government in everyday law-making. By analysing all legislation under the ordinary legislative procedure since 1999, this CEPS Explainer asks whether and – if so – how and how often the European Council refers to these specific laws in its summit conclusions. We show that the European Council mentions about 20% of all legislation and especially prioritises laws that redistribute money, expand EU competences and respond to a specific crisis. On prioritised laws, the European Council mandates the EU’s other institutions and Member States and acts actively and assertively across all stages of the policy process. This is the case even on priorities not shared with the European Commission as the legislative agenda-setter. In short, this Explainer puts the spotlight firmly on the national leaders who run the show – not just in large, visible summits but also during the follow-up everyday law-making process.
